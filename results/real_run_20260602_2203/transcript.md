## Turn 1 — con
While my opponent is right that AI coding agents reflect modern industry practice, *requiring* them in software engineering courses confuses a professional tool with a pedagogical one. The core purpose of these courses is to build the mental models beneath the code: how to decompose a problem, reason about data structures, trace control flow, and debug from first principles. When novices lean on an agent that produces plausible code instantly, they routinely skip exactly the productive struggle that forms those foundations. You cannot evaluate or correct what an agent generates if you never learned to write it yourself.

A mandate also wrongly assumes uniform readiness. Students arrive with vastly different backgrounds; forcing the same tool on everyone penalizes those who most need unmediated practice, while doing little for those already fluent. Industry adoption argues for *exposure*, not *compulsion* — graduates can learn agent workflows quickly once their fundamentals are sound, but cannot easily recover missing fundamentals later.

Finally, requiring agents raises real concerns about academic integrity, over-reliance, and the entrenchment of whatever biases or errors the tools carry. A far better policy lets instructors decide when AI assistance serves the learning objective, rather than mandating it across every course. Preparing students for industry means cultivating durable judgment first, not outsourcing the very skills the degree is meant to certify.

What endures is the ability to evaluate any tool critically—and you cannot critique what you never learned to do yourself. Voluntary, scaffolded exposure achieves readiness without forcing dependence.

There are also equity and integrity costs a requirement ignores. Mandated agents raise data-privacy questions, entrench reliance on commercial platforms, and make it far harder for instructors to assess genuine understanding. A blanket requirement removes the educator's judgment about *when* a tool helps and *when* it harms learning.

The goal is engineers who can reason independently and adopt new tools wisely. Compelling AI use undermines that goal. Universities should teach with these tools where pedagogically sound—never require them as a condition of learning.
